The Day-And-Date Experiment

10-15 min read

In reaction to the COVID-19 pandemic, some of Hollywood's top studios tinkered with theatrical distribution windows. Audiences were given the choice to stay home instead of purchasing a ticket to see a movie on the big screen. Did they want to?

By Jermy Francisco

Data Sources:

  • Google Trends
  • Box Office Mojo
  • The Movie Database API
What Defines A Movie's Success?

When a movie is released in theaters, a successful debut means the box-office ticket sales are higher than its costs. These ticket sales are publicly available and even used to promote the film as the "number 1 movie in America" — if they out-gross all others in a given week.

The metrics get more complicated when a movie is released for a streaming platform like a Netflix. We can ask how many subscribers (new and current) streamed the film? How many finished the movie? How many renewed their subscription? What portion of the subscription revenue (or ad revenue if you're a Hulu) can we attribute to the movie? Does that offset the costs of making the film? The answers are likely stuck behind company letterhead — unknown to you and I.

The Day-And-Date Dilemma

So when a movie comes out in theaters and on a streamer at the same time, how do we measure its impact?

Warner Bros. shocked the industry in December 2020 when it announced every WB theatrical release would be available day-and-date on HBO Max. Disney followed suit with releasing some movies on Disney Plus at a premier cost to subscribers. While it was more convenient for audiences, these strategies launched massive pushback from theater owners, directors, and actors concerned with their profits.

An interesting avenue would be to compare the Google Search popularity for planning to watch the movie in theaters vs at home. Considering that AMC is the largest theater-chain in America, and using Black Widow as an example, we can check the popularity of people searching for "Black Widow AMC", versus "Black Widow Disney Plus", and map it against the film's box-office ticket sales. If the movie did not release on a streamer, then I compared the search for "the movie + Watch Online" instead.

Warner Bros. And HBO Max

COVID-19 forced many theaters to close their doors in 2020. Theaters in major markets like NYC and LA remained closed through at least the Spring of 2021 — with many operating at limited capacity until later in the year. Before then, moviegoers were waiting for news that an effective vaccine would be available for them. For many adults, that news would not come before April.

Without knowing when COVID-19 restrictions would end and with cinema revenues cut short, it made sense for Warner Bros. to diversify their strategy. Seeing the opportunity to boost their new streaming platform, WB released their full 2021 theatrical slate on HBO Max and in theaters on the same day. While other major distributors who rely on ticket sales, like MGM or Sony, chose to delay their potential blockbusters, WB was able to shift their investments to HBO Max.

Because these movies would be free add-ons to current subscribers, it was likely more popular to stream the film versus going to the theater. With the exception of Godzilla vs Kong [2], there is a trend for a WB film's opening week. The Google Search interest in seeing a movie on HBO Max was more popular than searches for seeing the movie at AMC. Of note are the spikes in theater interest over streaming that occur after opening week — as seen in [2], [5], [7], [8], [9], and [10]. This likely reflects the portion of the audience who delayed their trip to the theater, possibly to avoid crowds during COVID.

Of course, there are other factors at play: the timing of a movie release, the audience demand for the movie, critical reception, word-of-mouth, competition from other releases, etc. Even so, it was likely the same-day streaming offer that imposed a ceiling on the box-office gross.

None of WB's 2021 films saw a domestic opening week north of $50M, averaging just $27.6M. The day-and-date strategy was likely seen as a success to WB as it propped up the HBO Max service. However, the fact that WB would announce the end of the day-and-date offering in 2022 suggests the desire to revitalize their theatrical business, without the competition from their own streaming service.

Interestingly, streaming search interest spiked past the 30-day mark for each film. HBO Max movies were day-and-date only for the first 30 days. This could reflect audiences that were unaware of the change, since prior to COVID, movies typically did not go to streaming until 60-90 days after release. To the detriment of their theatrical business, WB's strategy encouraged audiences to look at HBO Max first, and theaters second.

Disney Plus Premier Access

Early into the COVID-19 pandemic, Disney had chosen to delay their blockbusters and have the "smaller" titles skip theaters completely — releasing films like Raya and the Last Dragon and Soul straight to Disney Plus. Jungle Cruise [1] was Disney's first theatrical day-and-date release, only after theater capacity restrictions had opened up and the vaccination rate was rising. Disney saw what WB was attempting with HBO Max and took it one step further: charging a $30 premium for theatrical releases that would be available on Disney Plus "Premier Access".

In all three cases, the Google Search interest in watching the film on Disney Plus was higher than seeing it at AMC, during opening week. However, all three films did decently well at box-office: the average opening week was $64M. The average is heavily skewed up by Black Widow [3], which went on to be the year's fourth highest grossing title.

Like with WB's titles, the gap in popularity between searching for the movie on streaming and searching for it in AMC became much closer after the third or fourth week of release. When a movie releases theatrically, the majority of the film's ticket sales will come in the first three weeks. Cutting the window of theatrical exclusivity down from sixty days to zero meant that the box-office would take a hit.

Black Widow's sophomore week saw a ticket sale decline of 65%, the steepest second week fall for a Marvel movie. Considering the $30 premium, it is likely that Disney's offer did not attract a significant amount of new subscribers. Probably not enough to offset the loss in theatrical revenue, as Black Widow was the last title Disney chose to put on the day-and-date model for 2021.

Moviegoers Will Make The Trip For Event-Sized Films

With the exception of Black Widow, the rest of the 2021's top grossing titles (domestically) were all theatrical exclusives. As the charts above show, the interest in searching "the movie + AMC" had a lead over the search interest in watching these films online (a search that would include pirating the movie). With Spider-Man: No Way Home leading the pack with a massive opening week of $390M — and excluding Widow — the top films averaged an opening week of $115M, way more than the day-and-date titles.

What moviegoers are saying with their wallets is that if a movie is good enough, they will make the trip to the theaters. Although watching it at home may be more convenient, there is a clear preference to see the year's biggest movies on the big screen. Moviegoers have a demand for that event-like experience.

Looking Ahead

The quarantine period of COVID-19 allowed some of the industry's biggest distributors to experiment with the theatrical exclusivity window. At the time, it made sense to pivot from a theatrical model and boost the revenue coming in from streaming. As we entered the recovery phase of the pandemic, the day-and-date model became less lucrative. Moviegoers showed a willingness to return to the theater and a majority seem to prefer it over staying home.

Ultimately, both Disney and Warner Bros. joined their peers in moving to a 30-45 day theatrical exclusivity model. This gave the movie enough time in theaters to make a bulk of their ticket sales, while also shortening the time subscribers of streaming services had to wait to see a movie at home. The day-and-date strategy is likely not over for films that a studio may deem "minor" (Marry Me released in theaters and on Paramount Plus earlier 2022). However, studios are likely to be more careful with their major blockbusters — giving them more time in theaters before moving the offer to streaming.
